Bedfordshire Pledges to Be Carbon Neutral by 2087 “Give or Take a Decade”

By Angeline Marsh • 13 June 2026 • 3 min read
SATIRE — this article is a work of fiction created for comedic effect. Names, quotes, and events are invented.

The BESTS-2087 strategy document, photographed before the away-day in Milton Keynes. Image: illustrative.

In what has been described as the county's most ambitious environmental commitment to date, Bedfordshire has announced a target to achieve full carbon neutrality by 2087 — "or thereabouts," according to the strategy document's executive summary, "subject to available funding, technological development, political continuity, and what the weather is doing."

The Bedfordshire Environmental Sustainability Transition Strategy 2026–2087 (BESTS-2087), a 312-page document released last Tuesday, was the result of four years of cross-authority working groups, six public consultations, two independent reports, and what officers described as "an extremely productive away-day in Milton Keynes."

"We are proud to be setting out a clear, achievable pathway to a net-zero future," said the lead author of the strategy, Dr Philippa Holt-Ramsey. "Is 2087 ambitious? Absolutely. Is it the most ambitious timeline we considered? No — we also looked at 2095 and at 2150, but felt those perhaps lacked urgency."

The strategy contains 47 separate action points, ranging from the immediately actionable ("investigate the feasibility of switching council offices to LED lighting") to the more aspirational ("explore potential partnership with not-yet-invented carbon capture technologies, anticipated late 2070s").

Action Point 31, which covers agricultural land use across the county, notes that progress will depend on "continued engagement with the farming community, the cooperation of approximately 3,400 landowners, a supportive regulatory framework, and the resolution of several quite complex questions about soil carbon accounting that the scientific community has not yet fully agreed upon."

There is no contingency plan for if the scientific community fails to agree.

The public response to the strategy has been, charitably, mixed. Environmental campaigners welcomed the commitment in principle while noting that 61 years is "quite a long time," and that most of them would not personally be around to celebrate the target being met, missed, or quietly revised. Several asked whether there was a shorter-term milestone.

The strategy does include a 2040 checkpoint, at which point the council will "assess progress and, where necessary, update the framework to reflect evolving circumstances and any revisions to the science." Officers confirmed that "updating the framework" would involve another four-year working group process.

Members of the public attending the strategy's launch event in Bedford's Corn Exchange raised questions about more immediate concerns: solar panels on council buildings, the electric vehicle charging network, and the county's waste disposal emissions. One resident asked whether the county's landfill site was included in the strategy.

Page 247 addresses landfill. It acknowledges the issue and recommends a feasibility study.

The council's Finance Director confirmed that the strategy was "fully costed" for the period 2026–2032. For the period 2033–2087, costs had been "modelled indicatively" using assumptions about future technology prices, inflation, and policy — assumptions that, he acknowledged, "may not all prove accurate over a 55-year window."

The 312-page document is available on the council website. Officers are pleased to report that it was printed on recycled paper, though the 847 copies distributed to stakeholders represent, on one estimate, approximately 0.4 tonnes of CO‍₂.

The strategy includes no specific target for reducing the carbon footprint of council documentation.

A review of that gap is expected in 2028.
